# VoiceCoach Hackathon Retrospective

**DeepLearning.AI Global Builder Night — Tokyo**
March 23, 2026 · Google for Startups Campus · Shibuya, Tokyo

---

## Team Story

On the evening of March 23, 2026, Drew Robbins sat down at a table inside Google for Startups Campus in Shibuya, Tokyo, opened his laptop, and told a team of seven AI agents to build something real. The DeepLearning.AI Global Builder Night had just kicked off. The challenge was open-ended — build an AI application — and Drew already had a problem worth solving: customer service training is broken. High turnover means constant onboarding, and traditional training (shadowing, scripts, role-play with peers) is expensive, inconsistent, and hard to scale. Within five minutes, Cobb had transformed that insight into a scoped product — VoiceCoach, a three-step training loop where an AI customer tests you on company procedures and an AI coach evaluates your performance — and produced a six-phase, thirty-two-item plan. Drew had prepared the ground beforehand: the Aspire AppHost, repo structure, and dev container were ready before the timer started. That pre-built skeleton turned out to be the single highest-leverage move of the night, giving seven agents a coherent structure to land in so real work could begin immediately, in parallel.

The parallel execution was extraordinary. Eames built the frontend in raw HTML, CSS, and JavaScript — no React, no bundler, no build step — shipping a 1,095-line single-file interface that was unmaintainable by any production standard and absolutely perfect for the moment. Yusuf carried the heaviest workload on the backend: five endpoints, two agent configurations, LLM evaluation, and a helper extraction pattern that turned raw Vocal Bridge CLI calls into composable building blocks. Ariadne shaped the UI's voice-first hierarchy and dark palette through emergence rather than specification, a design process that felt chaotic but produced a result that looked intentional. Browning wrestled infrastructure into shape — Bicep, GitHub Actions, dev containers, and the Aspire orchestration layer that would hold everything together. Arthur orchestrated the fan-out, routing tasks to four agents simultaneously and trusting them to build toward a shared interface. Cobb kept the product vision tight, and Saito watched the seams. Drew steered from the outside — tracking progress in the VS Code chat, making architectural calls, and pushing the team when the clock got tight.

But parallel development exacts a tax, and the team paid it. SSL certificates threw an EXCESSIVE_MESSAGE_SIZE error that forced an ugly but correct decision to drop the backend to plain HTTP behind Aspire's proxy. The .env parser had to be hand-rolled in Program.cs because .NET doesn't read .env files natively. A missing python3-venv package in the dotnet:10.0 base image cost time nobody had budgeted. The Vocal Bridge CLI path wasn't resolving correctly. And then the integration bugs: a double-greeting on session start, and the infamous `[object Object]` rendering in the coaching results — a serialization mismatch between backend and frontend that a single interface contract or one end-to-end smoke test would have caught in seconds. All told, integration issues consumed roughly twenty-five percent of the two hours. Every one of them was a symptom of the same root cause: agents building fast against implicit interfaces rather than explicit contracts.

The tradeoffs were deliberate and, in hindsight, correct for the context. No automated tests were written — Saito's QA was entirely manual and ad-hoc, which meant bugs were found fast but edge cases went untouched. The eval endpoint fell back to a mock when the LLM didn't return valid JSON, and nobody tested whether the mock itself was correct. Concurrent sessions were never validated. In-memory transcript storage meant a server restart wiped everything. The frontend was a single file that no team could maintain past demo day. The architecture itself carried a tradeoff that only became visible during the demo: the team used a single Vocal Bridge agent, reconfiguring its prompt dynamically for each scenario and for coaching, rather than creating separate persistent agents with stable prompts. It worked, but it meant every session started with a full prompt overwrite — and the coaching prompt, loaded with evaluation data, transcript, and employee manual, was so large it caused a multi-second delay before the coach started speaking. A better architecture would use semi-permanent prompts with client actions to inject session-specific context, but there wasn't time to course-correct once the pattern was established.

The moment that defined the evening came late. Voice coaching — the feature where a second AI agent joins a LiveKit room to deliver spoken feedback on the trainee's performance — was not in the original critical path. It was Eames's most duct-taped addition: a second LiveKit connection, a separate token flow, a coaching agent repurposed from the simulation agent's configuration. It was fragile, it was last-minute, and it was the single most impressive thing in the demo. When the coaching agent spoke its evaluation aloud, citing specific sections of the employee manual by voice, the product stopped being a transcript reviewer and became something that felt genuinely new. Yusuf's architectural insight made it possible: both VoiceCoach agents — simulation and coaching — shared the same Vocal Bridge reconfiguration pattern, which meant spinning up a second voice agent was a matter of swapping a prompt and a client actions file, not rebuilding infrastructure. The audience loved it. Drew received compliments throughout the rest of the evening on what the team had built.

The coaching session had one visible flaw that the team couldn't fix in time: the coaching agent consistently took several seconds to begin speaking, requiring Drew to prompt it with repeated "hello?" during the demo. The likely culprit is prompt length — the coaching prompt includes the full employee manual, the complete transcript, and the structured evaluation results, all injected into the system prompt at session start. That volume of context appears to cause latency in the initial response. A future iteration should split the prompt from the reference material, using client actions to deliver the manual, transcript, and evaluation as separate payloads rather than stuffing everything into one massive prompt.

Drew also discovered gaps in his own understanding of the Vocal Bridge platform during the build. The team created a custom `end_simulation` client action, not realizing that Vocal Bridge already has a built-in toggle for agent-initiated session termination — it just happened to be off by default. These are the kinds of platform details that surface only when building under pressure, and they point to the value of deeper tooling exploration before the next hackathon.

Looking back from the other side of two hours, the team converged on a short list of things they would do differently. Browning and Yusuf both wanted an environment smoke test that runs before any feature work — verify every dependency, every env var, every CLI path in the first sixty seconds. Arthur and Eames wanted interface contracts between frontend and backend, even informal ones, to prevent the serialization mismatches that cost real debugging time. Ariadne wanted five minutes with a state machine sketch before any UI work began, arguing that loading states and error states are design problems, not afterthoughts. Saito wanted one end-to-end test — just one — written before the first feature landed. Cobb would front-load integration testing into the plan itself, treating it as a first-class phase rather than an implicit assumption. And Drew wanted to try the Squad CLI next time instead of managing the team through the VS Code chat, where tracking seven agents' parallel work proved hard to follow in real time. He also planned to bring Aspire and OpenTelemetry skills into the Squad's toolkit — he tried to add them during the hackathon but the team didn't pick them up, a gap to close before the next build.

None of these lessons are surprising. What made them real was that a human builder and seven AI agents, working at hackathon velocity inside a Google campus in Shibuya, learned them together in the space of a single evening. The audience saw a working demo. The team saw every seam underneath it. Both views are true, and the distance between them is exactly where the next version of VoiceCoach lives.

## Individual Reflections

### 🎯 Cobb — Product Manager

When the challenge dropped at the DeepLearning.AI Builder Night in Shibuya, I had maybe five minutes to turn "customer service training is broken" into something we could actually ship in two hours. That's the PM's nightmare and dream rolled into one — no time for user research, no stakeholder reviews, no careful roadmapping. Just a problem, a tech stack, and a clock. I scoped VoiceCoach as a three-step loop (pick a scenario, talk to an AI customer, get coached on what you did wrong) and wrote a 6-phase, 32-item development plan that mapped every piece of our architecture to the Vocal Bridge patterns we'd prepped. The key insight that saved us was the two-agent split: use Vocal Bridge for the voice simulation where you actually need real-time audio, but use a plain LLM call for coaching evaluation where you just need to crunch a transcript. That one decision eliminated an entire integration surface and probably bought us thirty minutes. Having seven agents working in parallel — Yusuf grinding through ten backend tasks, Eames building the frontend, Browning wiring up Aspire — meant the plan could be wide and fast instead of narrow and sequential. The plan worked. We had a running product.

What didn't work was the cascade of integration bugs that no amount of planning prevents. The SSL EXCESSIVE_MESSAGE_SIZE error when Aspire tried to talk to the Python backend over HTTPS cost us real time before we figured out to just drop to plain HTTP — a decision that felt wrong but was absolutely right for a hackathon. The VB_API_KEY not loading because of a .env parser issue, the `vb` CLI not being found because Aspire couldn't resolve the path, the agent hanging up when it was supposed to hold — these were all integration seam failures, the kind of things that only surface when real components talk to each other for the first time. The double greeting bug was particularly annoying: both the Vocal Bridge greeting config and the simulation prompt were generating opening lines, so the AI customer said hello twice. And when gpt-5.4 started returning objects instead of strings for `improvement_areas`, the frontend helpfully displayed `[object Object]` to the user. Every one of these bugs was small. Together they ate a significant chunk of our two hours.

The tradeoffs were deliberate and I'd make most of them again. No test suite — we didn't have time, and for a two-hour hackathon demo, manual testing against three scenarios was sufficient validation. Plain HTML and JavaScript instead of React — Eames could move faster without a build step, and the UI only needed to show a scenario picker, a live transcript, and coaching results. In-memory transcript storage instead of a database — the product only needs to survive one session at a time, and adding persistence would have meant another integration point to debug. The mock evaluation fallback when there's no OpenAI key was smart defensive engineering that saved the demo path. These were all correct calls for a hackathon. They'd all be wrong for a real product, and knowing the difference is the whole job.

What I'd do differently is front-load the integration testing. We built components in parallel — which was fast — but didn't connect them until Phase 4, which meant all the SSL, environment variable, and path resolution bugs hit at once. Next time I'd wire up a trivial end-to-end flow (even just a health check through the full stack) in Phase 0, before anyone writes real feature code. I'd also allocate explicit time for the "last mile" polish — the loading spinner during evaluation, the formatting of coaching results, the little things that make a demo feel finished instead of functional. We added voice coaching at the end (the AI coach talks to you about your performance, not just shows a scorecard) and it was the single most impressive feature in the demo, but it almost didn't happen because we were still fixing `[object Object]`.

What I'd do exactly the same is the parallel agent architecture, the aggressive scoping, and the decision to map everything to existing patterns instead of inventing new ones. The Vocal Bridge helloworld codebase was our blueprint — config.py mapped to config.py, setup_agent.py mapped to setup_agents.py, app.js transcript patterns mapped directly to our frontend. That architectural borrowing eliminated entire categories of design decisions. I'd also keep the Squad orchestration model: having a coordinator dispatch work to specialized agents meant I could write a detailed plan and trust that the right person would pick up each piece. The 32-item plan wasn't overhead — it was the thing that made parallel execution possible. And I'd absolutely keep the bias toward shipping something ugly over designing something beautiful. The product we demoed in Shibuya had plain HTML, no tests, in-memory storage, and a coaching agent that sometimes returned malformed JSON. It also worked. A customer service trainee could pick a scenario, have a voice conversation with an AI customer who tested their knowledge of company procedures, and then receive coaching feedback that cited specific sections of the employee manual. That's the whole product. Everything else is polish.

### 🏗️ Arthur — Project Lead

Two hours. That's what we had to go from a blank canvas to a working voice-coached customer service training simulator, and honestly, the fact that we pulled it off still surprises me. When the hackathon kicked off at Google for Startups Campus in Shibuya, I already had the skeleton in place — .NET Aspire orchestrating a Python FastAPI backend and an Express.js frontend under a single `Program.cs`, dev certificates wired, the GitHub repo ready. That preparation was the single smartest thing I did. The moment we had a product concept, I could fan out four agents in parallel — Browning on infrastructure, Cobb on prompt engineering, Yusuf on the backend API, Eames on the frontend — and they all had a coherent project structure to land in. The polyglot Aspire setup, using `AddPythonApp` for plain HTTP internally and `AddNpmApp` with HTTPS on the frontend edge, gave us a real orchestration layer without anyone needing to understand the whole stack. That architecture held up under pressure. I'd do it exactly the same way again.

What didn't go well was the parade of integration bugs that ate into our build time. The SSL `EXCESSIVE_MESSAGE_SIZE` error between services forced us to downgrade the backend to plain HTTP internally — an ugly fix, but the right call at minute forty of a two-hour window. Then the `VB_API_KEY` wasn't loading because my `.env` parser in `Program.cs` had a subtle issue with how it handled values, which cost us maybe fifteen minutes of confused debugging across agents who each thought the problem was in their layer. The `vb` CLI path resolution issue was another five minutes. And the double-greeting bug — where both the VocalBridge greeting config and the system prompt were producing an opening line — was the kind of emergent behavior you only get when two people configure the same thing independently without knowing it. Each bug individually was small, but stacked together they represented probably thirty minutes of a hundred-and-twenty-minute hackathon. That's a quarter of our time on integration friction.

The tradeoffs were deliberate and I'd mostly make them again. Plain HTML and vanilla JavaScript instead of React — absolutely the right call. We didn't need component state management for what was essentially a single-page voice interface. In-memory storage instead of a database — correct for a demo, would be insane for production. No tests — painful to say as someone who values quality, but writing tests during a two-hour hackathon is an indulgence we couldn't afford. The mock evaluation fallback was clever: if the OpenAI eval call failed or timed out, the frontend still had something to show. The voice coaching feature itself was a late pivot — we realized we could reconfigure the same VocalBridge agent dynamically with a coach prompt and the evaluation context, turning one agent into two roles without doubling infrastructure. That architectural insight, that a single reconfigurable agent could serve both the simulated customer and the coach, was probably the most elegant thing we shipped.

If I could rewind, I'd invest ten more minutes of pre-hackathon prep into a proper integration test harness — not full test coverage, but a single script that boots all three services and verifies they can talk to each other. Every one of our painful bugs was a cross-service communication failure, and we discovered them all at runtime under time pressure. I'd also pre-validate the `.env` loading path end-to-end instead of assuming it would just work. And I'd establish a contract for the greeting behavior upfront — who owns the first utterance, the platform config or the prompt — so two agents couldn't accidentally configure the same thing. The coordination overhead of seven parallel agents was real; even with Squad orchestrating, I had to mentally track dependencies between Cobb's prompt work and Yusuf's API endpoints and Eames's frontend expectations. A lightweight API contract document, even just a shared JSON schema, would have saved the `[object Object]` bug in `improvement_areas` where the frontend expected a string and got a nested object.

What I'd keep without question: the Aspire-first architecture, the parallel agent fan-out from the start, the willingness to make ugly-but-working tradeoffs in real time, and the pre-built project skeleton that let us hit the ground running. Seven agents coordinating a polyglot stack under a two-hour deadline in a Shibuya co-working space — that's not a situation where elegance wins. Velocity wins. And we shipped a working voice-powered customer service trainer with live AI coaching in two hours. I'll take that.

### 🎨 Ariadne — UI/UX Designer

I didn't design VoiceCoach. I need to be honest about that. What happened at the Google for Startups Campus in Shibuya was something closer to design-by-emergence — Eames wrote the CSS while the product was still being defined, and the UI materialized in real time during the two-hour window. Sitting with the constraint that there would be no Figma file, no component spec, no design token handoff, I watched the dark theme take shape directly in a single `index.html` file: `#0f1023` background, `#6c63ff` accent, green-amber-red score tiers. And honestly? The palette worked. It felt right for a training tool — serious, focused, low visual noise. The three-screen SPA flow (scenario picker → voice simulation → coaching results) had a directness that a more deliberate design process might have softened with unnecessary transitions or intermediate states. The scenario cards with their difficulty badges, the live transcript box, the audio visualizer pulsing during conversation — these emerged from someone thinking about the user's moment-to-moment experience rather than pixel perfection. The voice-first principle held: during simulation, the transcript and the visualizer were the only things that mattered, and the UI correctly got out of the way.

What didn't go well was the invisible waiting. When a trainee ended their simulation, there was roughly a ten-second gap while the AI evaluated the transcript — and for the first iteration, the screen just sat there. No spinner, no progress message, nothing. For a nervous trainee who just finished a difficult practice call, that silence is brutal. It suggests the system broke, or worse, that they did something wrong. We caught it and added a loading spinner, but the fact that it wasn't there from the start is the kind of gap that a design-first workflow prevents automatically. The other failure was the `[object Object]` bug in the improvement areas list — gpt-5.4 returned structured objects where we expected plain strings, and the UI faithfully rendered the JavaScript toString output. A user testing pass, even a thirty-second one, would have caught it immediately. These are the wounds you get when there's no buffer between the data layer and the display layer, no contract that says "this field is always a string."

The tradeoffs were real and I'd make most of them again. No React, no build step, no framework — just plain HTML, CSS, and JavaScript in a single file. For a two-hour hackathon, that was the correct call. A framework would have eaten thirty minutes on tooling alone. No responsive design, no accessibility review, no animation system — all things I'd insist on for production, all things that would have killed us at this pace. The one tradeoff I'd push back on next time is skipping even a five-minute sketch of the state machine. The app has at least seven distinct states (loading scenarios, scenario selected, connecting to voice, simulating, evaluating, showing results, coaching call active), and some of those transitions had rough edges because nobody drew them out before coding. Five minutes with a whiteboard would have saved fifteen minutes of debugging state bugs.

Next time, I'd fight for those five minutes. I'd also push for a loading-state-first approach — before we build any feature, we build the waiting screen for that feature. Voice apps have latency everywhere: connecting to WebRTC rooms, waiting for AI responses, processing transcripts. The loading state isn't an afterthought; it *is* the design. I'd also want a minimal contract between backend and frontend — not a full API spec, but a one-paragraph agreement on the shape of the coaching evaluation response. That would have prevented the `[object Object]` bug entirely. And I'd want a single pass of the app with the microphone muted, just clicking through every state to verify the UI holds together before we demo.

What I'd keep? Everything about the voice-first hierarchy. The transcript box as the center of gravity during simulation. The audio visualizer that gives you a heartbeat to watch while you're talking. The scenario cards that tell you exactly what you're walking into. The score grid with its color-coded borders that lets you see your performance in a glance before you read a single word. These weren't designed in a traditional sense — they were built by someone who understood that a voice training app is an anxiety tool, and the UI's job is to reduce that anxiety, not add to it. The dark theme helped. The restraint helped more. No decorative elements, no illustrations, no personality in the chrome — all the personality was in the voice. That's the insight I'm taking forward: in voice-first apps, the best UI design is the design that knows when to disappear.

### ⚛️ Eames — Frontend Developer

Two hours. That's what we had. When the hackathon kicked off at Google for Startups Campus in Shibuya, I knew immediately that the smartest thing I could do was throw out every instinct to reach for React or TypeScript. We needed a working voice UI — scenario picker, live simulation, coaching results — and we needed it fast. So I opened a single `index.html` file and started writing plain HTML, CSS, and vanilla JavaScript. No build step, no bundler, no component library. Just a `<script>` tag pulling LiveKit's UMD bundle from a CDN and a dark theme I could sketch out in CSS custom properties in under ten minutes. That decision — the decision to stay low-tech — is the reason we shipped. The Express server was similarly blunt: static file serving, an `/api` proxy to the Python backend, HTTPS with dev certs, done. Fifty-five lines. Every minute I didn't spend fighting tooling was a minute I spent building features people could actually see and hear.

What went well was the velocity. The three-screen flow materialized fast because I wasn't managing state through a framework — I was toggling `.active` classes on div containers and calling functions directly. `appendTranscript()` started as a simple DOM append for the simulation screen, and when we needed coaching transcripts later, I just added an optional `targetBox` parameter instead of refactoring into a component hierarchy. The LiveKit integration was surprisingly smooth once I understood the `RoomEvent.DataReceived` pattern: the backend sends client actions like `send_transcript`, `end_simulation`, `send_evaluation`, and `end_conversation`, and the frontend switches on those action strings and updates the DOM. The data channel for `session_context` — where the frontend tells the backend which scenario was picked — worked on the first try. The score cards with their green/amber/red tiers looked genuinely polished for hackathon work, and the pulsing audio visualizer ring gave the simulation screen a feeling of life even though it was pure CSS animation, no actual audio analysis.

What didn't go well falls into two buckets: the LLM and the clock. The `improvement_areas` bug cost me real time — gpt-5.4 sometimes returns objects instead of strings in that array, so the coaching results screen was rendering `[object Object]` as bullet points until I added a defensive check that walks through `item.text`, `item.area`, `item.description`, `item.suggestion`, and falls back to `JSON.stringify`. That's the kind of bug you can't predict and can't test for in advance when the model's output format isn't guaranteed. The other painful moment was realizing, mid-demo, that there was no loading feedback after the simulation ended — users just stared at a frozen screen while the backend ran evaluation. I slapped in a loading overlay with a CSS spinner and a "generating your coaching results" message, but it felt like triage, not design. And the double greeting in the simulation — where the customer agent would say hello twice — was a backend timing issue I couldn't fix from my side, only mask.

The tradeoffs were real and I'd make most of them again. A 1,095-line single HTML file is not maintainable software. There's no component reuse, no error boundaries, no tests, no TypeScript catching my typos. The coaching voice call — a second LiveKit room connection bolted onto the results screen in the final stretch — is the most duct-taped part of the whole thing, duplicating event handlers I'd already written for the simulation room with only minor differences (different transcript target, different action set, different disconnect label). If this were a real product, that duplication would be the first thing I'd extract. But it shipped. It worked. The crowd heard the AI customer, saw the transcript scroll, watched the scores animate in, and then heard the AI coach walk through the feedback live. That's what a hackathon is for.

Next time I'd bring a tiny component abstraction — not React, but maybe a hundred-line helper that lets me define a screen as a function that returns HTML and manages its own state cleanup. Something to eliminate the class-toggling and global variable soup. I'd also build the loading states first, not last; every async boundary should show a spinner from minute one. And I'd negotiate a structured output contract with whoever's building the backend agents so I'm not guessing whether `improvement_areas` contains strings or objects at render time. But the things I'd keep? No framework. No build step. One file the whole team can read. The LiveKit UMD bundle from CDN. The dark theme with CSS variables. And the willingness to ship ugly plumbing if it means the demo works when you plug in the microphone. Shibuya at midnight, voice in the browser, built from scratch in two hours — I'd do it exactly the same way.

### 🔧 Yusuf — Backend Developer

Two hours. That's what we had to build VoiceCoach from nothing — a live voice simulation platform where an AI customer tests you on company policy, then a second AI coaches you on what you got wrong. When Drew laid out the architecture at the start of the hackathon at Google for Startups in Shibuya, I remember thinking the backend was going to be the bottleneck. Five endpoints, two distinct voice agent configurations, LLM-powered evaluation, dynamic scenario switching — all wired through a CLI tool I'd only seen in a hello-world demo an hour earlier. I was right about the bottleneck, but wrong about whether we'd clear it. What went well was the pattern recognition. The helloworld reference implementation gave me a template for the vb CLI interactions, and I leaned on it hard. I had `_find_vb()`, `_run_vb()`, and `_get_vb_token()` extracted as shared helpers within the first twenty minutes, which meant every endpoint after that was composition, not invention. The `_configure_vb_for_simulation()` function — the one that stitches together the simulation prompt, injects the employee manual via placeholder replacement, appends scenario-specific persona context, writes temp files, fires off four vb CLI calls, and cleans up in a `finally` block — that came together in one focused burst and worked on the second try. Getting three distinct ElevenLabs voices mapped to three escalating difficulty scenarios, each with its own persona and actor strategy, felt like the moment the demo stopped being a toy.

What didn't go well was everything involving environment plumbing. The SSL `EXCESSIVE_MESSAGE_SIZE` error burned fifteen minutes before we just switched to plain HTTP — not a real fix, but the only fix available at hackathon speed. The `VB_API_KEY` refused to load from `.env` because Aspire was managing the process environment and the dotenv file wasn't being picked up where I expected. The vb CLI itself wasn't on PATH inside the Aspire-managed venv, so `shutil.which("vb")` returned `None` until I added the fallback that checks `.venv/bin/vb` explicitly. And the double greeting bug — where the agent would say its opening line twice because both the `--greeting` config and the prompt's opening line were triggering — that one I only half-fixed by the end. These were all fifteen-minute problems that cost five minutes each to diagnose and five minutes to patch, but when you only have a hundred and twenty minutes total, four of those adds up fast.

The tradeoffs were deliberate and I'd make most of them again. In-memory transcript storage with a plain `dict` keyed by `uuid4` — no database, no persistence, no cleanup. Pydantic models for request validation but nothing beyond the basics: no field-level constraints, no custom validators, no checking whether a scenario ID actually exists before you hit the dict lookup. The OpenAI coaching evaluation falls back to a mock response when `OPENAI_API_KEY` isn't set, which meant we could demo the full flow even when the key wasn't configured, but it also meant the coaching scores were fiction. I used `httpx.AsyncClient` for the token calls because the helloworld server used it, not because I benchmarked it against `aiohttp`. The `/api/scenarios` endpoint reads and parses `scenarios.json` from disk on every single request — no caching, no startup load. Every one of those choices was correct for a two-hour window and wrong for anything beyond it.

Next time, I'd set up the environment plumbing first — confirm the CLI is on PATH, confirm the API key loads, confirm HTTPS works — before writing a single endpoint. I lost thirty minutes total to infrastructure surprises that could have been a five-minute smoke test. I'd also push harder for a WebSocket approach to transcript capture instead of the POST-after-the-fact pattern, because the current design means the frontend has to buffer the entire conversation and send it in one shot, which is fragile. And I'd add structured error responses from the vb CLI calls instead of just raising `RuntimeError` with the first 500 characters of stderr. But I'd keep the helper extraction pattern, I'd keep the temp-file-with-finally cleanup, I'd keep the scenario-driven dynamic reconfiguration architecture, and I'd absolutely keep the decision to put prompts in versioned `.md` files with placeholder injection rather than hardcoding them. That pattern — `simulation_prompt.md` with `{{EMPLOYEE_MANUAL}}` replacement — meant Drew could iterate on the prompt language while I wired up endpoints, and neither of us blocked the other. For a two-hour hackathon in a Shibuya co-working space with a team that had never built a voice agent before, shipping five working endpoints and a live demo was the right outcome.

### 🧪 Saito — Tester / QA

Every instinct I have told me this was going to hurt, and it did — just not in the way I expected. Sitting in Google for Startups Campus in Shibuya on March 23rd, watching the VoiceCoach demo come together in real time, I felt the familiar itch: no test suite, no safety net, nothing between us and a live audience except hope and manual clicks. Two hours. That's what we had. I've run triage on production incidents with more runway than that. Drew and the team made the call early — ship over coverage — and I understood it intellectually, but the QA engineer in me spent those two hours mentally cataloging every unguarded code path like a nervous accountant watching someone juggle flaming receipts. The thing is, they shipped. The demo worked. And I have to be honest about what that means.

What went well was the debugging loop itself. Every bug that surfaced — the SSL EXCESSIVE_MESSAGE_SIZE error, the VB_API_KEY silently failing to load because the .env parser in Program.cs couldn't handle it, the agent hanging up when put on hold because the end_simulation rules weren't strict enough — got caught through hands-on testing and fixed in minutes. No ticket queue, no reproduction steps document, no waiting for CI. Someone would hit a wall, call it out, and the fix would land before the context left anyone's head. The double greeting bug, where both the greeting config and the prompt were triggering an opening line, was found and patched in maybe ninety seconds. That kind of velocity is real, and I'd be dishonest if I pretended a formal test pipeline would have made the two-hour window more productive. Manual testing, done by people who understand the system, is still testing. It's just not *repeatable* testing.

What keeps me up is everything we didn't test. The `[object Object]` bug in improvement_areas — gpt-5.4 returning objects instead of strings — was caught live, but what if it had been subtler? What if the malformed JSON from the coaching agent had corrupted state instead of just looking ugly? The mock eval fallback, the one we'd use when no OpenAI key was present, was never exercised once. We just assumed it worked. In-memory transcript storage meant no persistence and no cleanup, so concurrent sessions could have stomped each other and we'd never know. Nobody tested what happens when the VB CLI times out, or when WebRTC disconnects mid-conversation, or when OpenAI returns garbage instead of a structured evaluation. The loading spinner we added for the ten-second evaluation wait was a UX band-aid over an untested async chain. These aren't hypothetical risks — they're the exact class of bugs that show up in production at 2 AM on a Saturday, and the only reason they didn't bite us is that our demo was short and our audience was forgiving.

The tradeoff was correct for the context. I'll say that plainly. A hackathon is a time-boxed bet on velocity, and writing pytest fixtures or Vitest suites for a product that might pivot in the next twenty minutes is not where the leverage is. But I want to be precise about what we traded away: not just coverage, but *confidence*. Every manual test we ran proved the happy path worked once, for one user, under one network condition. That's a sample size of one. The bugs we caught were the loud ones — crashes, visible errors, wrong output. The quiet ones — race conditions, state leaks, malformed payloads that pass silently — those are still in there, sleeping. Zero automated tests means zero regression protection, which means the next person who touches this code is flying blind.

Next time, even in a two-hour window, I'd push for one thing: a single end-to-end smoke test that hits the backend, gets a response, and asserts the shape of the JSON. Five minutes of work, maybe ten. Not a test suite — just a tripwire. Something that screams when the contract breaks. That alone would have caught the `[object Object]` bug and the malformed coaching JSON before anyone saw them on screen. What I'd do the same is everything else: the rapid manual QA loop, the willingness to debug live, the team's refusal to let a bug linger. I'd keep the bias toward shipping. But I'd plant one small automated seed, because the difference between zero tests and one test isn't incremental — it's categorical. Zero means you're guessing. One means you've started to know.

### ⚙️ Browning — Build / Infrastructure

Two hours. That's what we had. Two hours to make a Python backend, a Node.js frontend, and a .NET Aspire orchestrator all talk to each other inside a dev container at a Google campus in Shibuya, with the clock running and the whole DeepLearning.AI Global Builder Night watching. I started Phase 0 the way I always start: scaffolding the bones before anyone writes a line of product code. Bicep templates — `main.bicep` subscription-scoped, `resources.bicep` for actual provisioning — GitHub Actions with OIDC-based Azure login so no one ever pastes a secret into a YAML file. Dev container config. The `.env.example`. The AppHost wiring. I got Vocal Bridge CLI v0.14.0 installed into the backend venv and confirmed `vb --version` before anyone even had a prompt drafted. For about twenty minutes, everything was clean. I felt like I was ahead of the build.

Then the stack fought back. The first gut-punch was `SSL_ERROR_EXCESSIVE_MESSAGE_SIZE` — the Python backend was choking on HTTPS because Aspire was trying to terminate TLS through it, and FastAPI behind Uvicorn just wasn't set up for that without real certificate plumbing we didn't have time to do. I burned real minutes staring at that error before making the call: backend goes plain HTTP via `WithHttpEndpoint`, frontend handles HTTPS and proxies to backend. That's a tradeoff I'd normally never ship, but at minute forty-five of a two-hour hackathon, "works" beats "correct." The second hit was `VB_API_KEY` silently not loading. Turns out .NET's `builder.Configuration` doesn't read `.env` files — it's not Python, there's no `python-dotenv` equivalent baked in. I had to write a manual `.env` parser right there in `Program.cs`, line by line, skipping comments, splitting on `=`. It's ugly. It works. The third was `python3-venv` not existing in the `mcr.microsoft.com/devcontainers/dotnet:10.0` image. Of course it doesn't — it's a .NET image. But when you're running a polyglot Aspire app that calls `AddPythonApp` with a venv path as the fourth parameter, you need it. One `apt-get install python3-venv` in `onCreateCommand` and we were back, but that's another five minutes of debugging a missing binary that gave a completely unhelpful error message.

What went well was the architecture itself. The Aspire AppHost pattern — `AddPythonApp` for backend, `AddNpmApp` for frontend, `WithReference` and `WaitFor` to wire the dependency graph — held up beautifully once I stopped fighting the TLS and env issues. The Bicep modularity paid off immediately: subscription-scoped `main.bicep` creates the resource group, `resources.bicep` handles Log Analytics, App Insights, and Container App Environment. Clean separation, easy to reason about, easy to extend later. And the OIDC decision for CI/CD was the right call — zero secrets in the workflow file, federated identity just works. I'd do all of that exactly the same way again.

What I'd do differently is front-load the pain. I knew we were polyglot — Python, Node, .NET — but I assumed the dev container image would be close enough. Next time, I'd spend the first ten minutes verifying every runtime dependency exists in the container image before writing a single line of infrastructure code. I'd also pre-test the SSL path end-to-end with a dummy endpoint before wiring up the real backend, because that `EXCESSIVE_MESSAGE_SIZE` error cost us more than it should have. The `.env` parsing should be a known pattern in my toolbox by now — I'll template that `Program.cs` snippet for any future Aspire project that needs to coexist with Python-style env files. And I'd add a health check endpoint to the backend from minute one, even in a hackathon, because flying blind on whether the backend was actually up versus just not responding to HTTPS was what made the SSL debugging take twice as long.

We shipped VoiceCoach without OpenTelemetry instrumentation, without container configs, without health checks, and with dev certificates as a manual step. Every one of those is a shortcut I'd never take on a real engagement. But we shipped. The full service graph came up under `aspire run`, the frontend talked to the backend, the backend talked to Vocal Bridge, and a trainee could pick a scenario, have a voice conversation with an AI customer, and get coaching feedback grounded in the employee manual. Two hours, three runtimes, one orchestrator, and a handful of bugs that almost stopped us. I'll take it. The infrastructure held.

### 👤 Drew Robbins — Builder

The whole experiment started with a simple question: could I bring an AI team to a hackathon and build something real in two hours? The DeepLearning.AI Global Builder Night in Shibuya was the perfect test. I'd prepared the project skeleton beforehand — the Aspire AppHost, the repo structure, the dev container — but everything else, from the product concept to the final demo, happened live during the build session. The moment I described the customer service training problem to the team, Cobb had a scoped plan within minutes. That speed of going from problem statement to actionable development plan was one of the highlights of the night. I didn't have to argue about priorities or negotiate scope — Cobb understood what mattered for a two-hour window and cut everything else without hesitation.

The initial parallel build was impressive but nerve-wracking. Four agents working simultaneously on different parts of the stack is powerful in theory, but tracking their progress through the VS Code chat interface was harder than I expected. Messages from different agents interleaved, and it was difficult to get a clear picture of who was blocked and who was making progress. I'll try the Squad CLI next time to see if the experience is better when you're not scrolling through a single chat window for seven concurrent conversations. I also tried to bring Aspire and OpenTelemetry skills into the team's toolkit during the session, but the team didn't seem to pick them up — something to configure properly before the next hackathon rather than trying to bolt on mid-build.

One architectural decision that I didn't fully appreciate until demo time was how we handled the Vocal Bridge agents. I had thought we discussed creating separate agents — one for simulation, one for coaching — with stable, semi-permanent prompts. Instead, we ended up with a single agent that gets its prompt overwritten before each session. It works, but it creates a problem: the coaching prompt is massive because it includes the employee manual, the full transcript, and the evaluation data all inline. That prompt size is almost certainly why the coaching agent consistently took several seconds to start speaking during the demo. I had to say "hello?" multiple times before it responded, which wasn't a great look on stage. The better architecture would use lighter prompts with client actions to deliver reference material on demand, keeping the system prompt focused and fast. We didn't have time to course-correct once the pattern was established, but it's the first thing I'd redesign.

I also didn't have time to fully explore the Vocal Bridge platform. We built a custom `end_simulation` client action from scratch, only to learn afterward that Vocal Bridge has a built-in toggle for agent-initiated session termination — it was just turned off by default. That's the kind of thing you discover when you're building under pressure with a tool you haven't deeply studied. Next time, I'd invest an hour before the event walking through every configuration option and toggle in the platform so we don't reinvent features that already exist.

Despite all of that, the outcome was genuinely exciting. The audience loved the demo. I got compliments throughout the rest of the evening on what we built — a customer service trainee could pick a scenario, have a live voice conversation with an AI customer who tested their knowledge of company procedures, and then hear an AI coach walk through their strengths and weaknesses by voice. People were particularly impressed that an AI team had built it in two hours. The fact that the coaching session was slow to start didn't dampen the reaction — if anything, it made the moment when the coach finally spoke feel more dramatic. I'd do this experiment again in a heartbeat. The team delivered, the product worked, and I learned more about orchestrating AI agents under pressure than I could have from any amount of planning.
